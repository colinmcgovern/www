HbA1c Peak Analysis Library

At Sepax Technologies, I wrote the algorithm for our GlyHb-100 machine to turn patient data into a diabetes diagnosis. The GlyHb-100 queues up to hundreds of samples of blood and it one-by-one it runs each sample through one of our HPLC columns. HPLC columns separate the chemicals that a sample is composed of by its properties, for example ionic charge or size. By measuring the absorbance of the sample as it comes out of a column, a chromatagraph is formed. This chromatagraph is a multimodal distrubution. My algorithm separates each distrubution, called a peak, out of the chromatagraph. By comparing the areas of each peak, we can find the a1c %, which tells us whether or not a sample is postive for diabetes.

Challenges with this project were:
1. Finding the peak areas accurately. The area ratio results need to be within 0.01% of the actual value.
2. Labeling the peaks with the correct blood marker names.
3. Making the library rarely fail and never crash.
4. Having the average latency of the algorithm be around 2000 ms. This was especially difficult, because fitting the curve's model formula to the sample points needs non-linear regression.
5. Having the results be repeatable. The same sample ran mulitple times should yeild the same result.
